# Point Social Creative Patterns

Use these patterns as starting points, not formulas. Vary the homeowner tension, insight, proof, visual device, and emotional payoff so the result does not become cosmetic reskins.

## Concept axes

| Axis | Options |
| --- | --- |
| Homeowner need | cash-flow relief, debt management, repairs, a major expense, retirement, caregiving, self-employment, business investment, flexible project funding, uncertainty about options |
| Product story | HEI, HELOC, dual-product choice, eligibility or prequalification, process, comparison, customer outcome |
| Proof | product mechanic, approved number, demonstration, customer quote, expert explanation, comparison dimension, local relevance, interface or offer preview |
| Format | UGC, testimonial, static, subtle loop, short animation, product demo, text conversation, carousel, creator explainer, comparison card |
| Emotional payoff | breathing room, control, clarity, possibility, preparedness, confidence, flexibility, momentum |
| Visual device | home scene, everyday object, interface, text bubbles, before-and-after state, split path, metaphor, seasonal scene, ambient illustration |
| Test variable | hook, tension, proof, spokesperson, format, pacing, CTA, product framing, visual device |

## Reusable territories

### Outcome-led environment

Place the homeowner in a calm, recognizable setting that embodies the benefit. Use restrained motion or one visual metaphor. Keep the financial idea legible instead of burying it in lifestyle imagery.

### Product-choice story

Show that different situations may call for different products. Use two households, one household considering options, or a branching structure. Keep the choice consultative and homeowner-driven.

### Native UGC

Start with a believable homeowner tension in direct-to-camera language. Move from problem to discovery to product fit to next step. Let on-screen text summarize the product truth without audio.

Avoid polished spokesperson delivery, exaggerated reactions, generic stock-home footage, and institutional-sounding dialogue.

### Testimonial proof

Organize approved quotes around a recognizable use case or emotional payoff. Hold each quote long enough to read, preserve attribution requirements, and use motion only to support legibility.

Never invent, merge, clean up, or repurpose a testimonial without permission and review.

### Helpful comparison

Compare products or alternatives on a small number of homeowner-relevant dimensions. Use neutral language, parallel structure, and clear qualification. Avoid declaring Point right for everyone.

### Interface-native storytelling

Use text messages, search, calculator, offer preview, comments, or another familiar interface to reveal the story one beat at a time. Keep natural pacing and large readable type. Do not turn the execution into a meme.

### Visual mnemonic

Choose one object that makes the benefit memorable: a stopwatch for speed, a keyhole for access, a coin for value, or a weather object for preparedness. Animate one or two properties while keeping the logo, headline, legal copy, and layout stable.

### Seasonal wrapper

Use a seasonal setting for relevance while preserving the product message. Keep the treatment restrained and brand-led. Avoid urgency, gimmicks, casino cues, or a theme that overwhelms the reason to believe.

### Process or product demo

Demonstrate the next step, prequalification experience, or product interaction. Show only the actions needed to make the benefit credible. Use current approved UI and claims.

### Proof-led localization

Use an approved local proof point, homeowner scenario, or property context. Validate every number and do not imply an individual outcome from an aggregate figure.

## Motion defaults

- Design for silent viewing first.
- Establish the message within the first three seconds.
- Keep motion subordinate to comprehension.
- Prefer ambient motion, a single kinetic object, or clear sequential storytelling.
- Keep headline, logo, legal copy, and CTA fixed when motion is ambient.
- Make loops seamless and free of obvious mechanical repetition.
- Avoid rapid flashing, excessive emoji, chaotic transitions, and reduced reading time.
- Build cutdowns intentionally; do not merely trim a longer asset.

## UGC defaults

- Lead with a specific, conversational tension.
- Use real-feeling settings and behavior.
- Let the speaker explain why an option fit their situation.
- Use concise captions and product cards for essential proof only.
- Preserve multiple valid homeowner paths.
- End with a low-pressure next step.

## Differentiation test

Treat ideas as duplicates when they share the same tension, promise, proof, format, and payoff even if the headline or scene changes. Require each idea to change at least three strategic axes.

Balance the slate across human and graphic-led concepts, education and emotion, product and outcome, low and moderate production effort, and proven versus exploratory formats.

## Common failure modes

- Starting with the product instead of the homeowner situation.
- Turning one concept into six headline variants.
- Using a metaphor that requires explanation.
- Packing both product mechanics into a short unit without a simple takeaway.
- Treating testimonial copy as generic ad copy.
- Using historical numbers or old legal language as current truth.
- Letting art, motion, or creator personality overpower the message.
- Omitting a first-frame hook, legal-safe area, or test hypothesis.
